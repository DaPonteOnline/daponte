About us¶

Da Ponte Libretti is a digital critical edition of late seventeenth- and eighteenth-century Italian drammi per musica, built around the corpus of Lorenzo Da Ponte (1749–1838) and its hypotexts. The platform is a bilingual static site (Italian primary, English secondary) that provides for each edition a reading view, a critical apparatus where multiple witnesses exist, and a multi-way comparison for the lines of genetic filiation.

The current corpus comprises 6 editions across 4 distinct texts. The project’s flagship view is the three-way comparison of Il talismano, which juxtaposes Goldoni’s 1779 version, the anonymous revision of the same year, and Da Ponte’s 1788 rewriting.

The editions¶

All editions are critical in nature (not diplomatic): they do not reproduce the typographic layout of the page, and they regularise mechanical errors and orthography according to criteria declared in the <teiHeader>. Each one has a specific editorial focus.

Cinna by Da Ponte (London 1798)
Da Ponte's rewriting of the Cinna by Angelo Anelli (Milan 1792, music by Bonifazio Asioli, La Scala 1793). Da Ponte's libretto, set to music by Francesco Bianchi, was staged at the King's Theatre in London in 1798. The interest of the edition lies in foregrounding Da Ponte's modus operandi on the model: what he keeps, what he cuts, what he reworks; the side-by-side view of the two texts is designed precisely to make minimal departures, partial rewritings and structural interventions perceptible. Edited by Ilaria Scarponi.
Il ricco d'un giorno by Da Ponte (Vienna 1784)
Dramma giocoso in three acts, music by Antonio Salieri, first performed at the Burgtheater in Vienna on 6 December 1784. Edition based on a single witness (Bologna, Museo Internazionale e Biblioteca della Musica, Lo.02886); other states of the same edition, held in European and American libraries, are listed but not collated. The edition aims to make critical text, hypotexts and parallel passages comparable, in support of analyses of the author's modus scribendi. Edited by Alessandro Malavasi.
Il talismano (Milan 1779 / Vienna 1788)
Three-witness edition comparing three interrelated textual layers: Carlo Goldoni's original libretto (Milan 1779, music by Antonio Salieri and Giacomo Rust); the Mutazioni of the anonymous reviser (pp. 87–116 of the same witness); and Lorenzo Da Ponte's rewriting (Vienna 1788, music by Antonio Salieri), which incorporates both the original and the revisions. The three-way comparison view is the project's flagship view: to represent the progressive integration of the revisions into the final redaction and to make the genetic trajectory of the text visible. Edited by Marta Pacchin.

Editorial method¶

Every edition in the corpus is encoded in TEI P5 from an identified printed witness described in <sourceDesc>. Encoding covers the poetic text verse by verse, stage directions, the cast list (<castList>) and — where declared — metrical annotation.

Critical apparatus¶

The apparatus is not hand-coded in the TEI files: it is generated automatically by CollateX from the collation of the witnesses declared in the corpus manifest. The result is standoff markup in <app>/<lem>/<rdg> kept separate from the source texts. This makes it possible to regenerate the apparatus deterministically whenever an encoding is updated.

Three-way comparison¶

For the Talismano, the pipeline produces an intertextual alignment that assigns each segment of Da Ponte’s text a provenance code (verbatim from Goldoni, modified, verbatim from the Anonymous reviser, paraphrase, innovation, omission…). Eight codes in total, marked visually by a coloured border on the spine column plus a textual label. The current alignment is an automated draft (cert=medium); an editor-curated version will replace the draft in a future release.

Data transparency¶

Every step of the pipeline produces deterministic output. The source TEI files, intermediate JSON, and standoff alignment files are all committed to git, so that every editorial change is diffable by anyone reviewing the repository. TEI validation runs on every build and produces a per-file health report.

How to cite¶

The recommended citation form is generated automatically from the CITATION.cff file at the repository root, following the Citation File Format standard. GitHub exposes it via the “Cite this repository” button; every release deposits a permanent DOI on Zenodo.

Example (BibTeX)¶

@misc{DaPonteLibretti,
title  = {Da Ponte Libretti — digital edition},
author = {Borin, Alessandro and Levchenko, Mariia and Malavasi, Alessandro
          and Pacchin, Marta and Priore, Roberta and Scarponi, Ilaria},
year   = {2026},
url    = {https://mary-lev.github.io/DaPonte/},
note   = {DOI: 10.5281/zenodo.XXXXXXX (current version),
          10.5281/zenodo.YYYYYYY (concept)}
}

Example (Chicago, author-date)¶

Borin, Alessandro, Mariia Levchenko, Alessandro Malavasi, Marta Pacchin, Roberta Priore, and Ilaria Scarponi. 2026. Da Ponte Libretti — digital edition. https://mary-lev.github.io/DaPonte/. DOI: 10.5281/zenodo.XXXXXXX.

Citing a single edition¶

Each reading view exposes a unique permalink for every verse (for example, #l_a1_s1_001) and will provide an ad-hoc per-edition citation through the “Cite” dialog in the header in upcoming releases.

Hosting and infrastructure¶

Static site generated with Astro; TEI encoding processed with lxml + pydantic; collation via CollateX (pure Python); full-text search with Pagefind; facsimile viewer with OpenSeadragon. Sources on GitHub, deployed on GitHub Pages.

Contact¶

For editorial or technical issues, open an issue on the GitHub repository. For citation, attribution or collaboration requests, contact the editors through the University of Bologna.
